Polymer Waste Upcycling

Polymers are ubiquitous materials produced at a large scale. The non-degradable polymer waste with low recycling rate poses severe environmental threats. We investigate catalytic reactions converting polymer waste back into the monomers or other petroleum-based chemicals, to achieve a circular economy. We specifically tackle challenges rising from the unique properties of the polymer feedstocks. Two sub-themes of the project are: 1) tailoring the bifunctionality of transition-metal-carbide surfaces for impurity-compatiable hydrocracking; 2) leveraging precise polymer characterization and synthesis capabilities to decipher and facilitate upcycling kinetics.

Rigorous Structure-Function Relationship

Many heterogeneous catalysts have metal nanoparticles as the active species. The size, shape, and density of the nanoparticles determine their reactivity. However, these structural parameters often change simultaneously in catalyst synthesis, and thus their effects on catalysis (“structure-function relationship”) are difficult to cleanly elucidate.

Inspired by the epitaxial growth theory of self-assembled quantum dots, we developed a framework that tunes each structural parameter independently. The correlation between particle density and size is broken with the size-limited growth of strained epitaxial islands. Through broad collaboration, we use in-situ spectroscopy (X-ray absorption spectroscopy, XAS, probe-IR, low-energy ion scattering, LEIS) and scanning transmission electron miscroscopy (STEM) to model the structure of the metal particles. The platform is deployed to study reactions involving inter-particle phenomenon, allowing us to rigorously establish structure-function relationships, the foundation for rational catalyst design.

Sub-Nanometer Metal Catalysts

Sub-nanometer metal clusters (Mn) and single metal atoms (M1) are desired for catalysis due to the unique electronic properties and high fractions of active sites. However, these species easily sinter under reaction conditions, and lack the functionality to catalyze many complicated reactions. We attempt to use reducible nano-oxide on irreducible oxide support as functional anchors for Mn/M1. The discrepancy between adhesion energy of M1 on the anchor and support creates kinetic barriers that prevents sintering. The anchor also relieves the catalytic duty of M1/Mn by taking on elementary steps occurring slowly on them. This strategy will be applied to the mechanism-informed catalyst design for H2 storage/release and selective CO2 conversion. The dynamics of Mn/M1 will be tracked with in-situ spectroscopy.​

Biomass and CO2 Utilization

Currently, the chemical industry heavily relies on petroleum-based carbon feedstocks, representing an unsustainable model with significant carbon emission and footprint. We are interested in developing new processes and catalysts that integrate bio-resources (such as lignin) and CO2 into existing chemical manufacturing routes as renewable feedstocks, enabling the production of carbon-neutral/negative platform chemicals (such as styrene and ethanol). We aim at establishing new conversion routes, designing efficient catalysts, and analyzing the footprints of the processes.​
